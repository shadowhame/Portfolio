Ursula K. Le Guin

American writer, renowned for her groundbreaking works in speculative fiction

Le Guin in 1995

A timeline of Le Guin's life:

  • 1929 - Born in Berkeley, California, on October 21
  • 1951 - Received B.A. in Renaissance French and Italian literature at Radcliffe College of Harvard University
  • 1952 - Earned a M.A. in French at Columbia University
  • 1953 - Married historian Charles Le Guin in Paris
  • 1957 - Gave birth to her first daughter
  • 1958 - Moved to Portland, Oregon
  • 1959, 1964 - Gave birth to a daughter and a son
  • 1966 - Rocannon's World, her first published novel, was released
  • 1968-1969 - Garnered widespread acclaim for her two science fiction novels, A Wizard of Earthsea and The Left Hand of Darkness
  • 1970 - Became the first woman to win the Hugo and Nebula Awards for best novel (The Left Hand of Darkness)
  • 1973 - Won the Hugo Award for her novel The Word for World is Forest, which Le Guin later described as the "most over political statement" amongst any of her work (the novel was inspired by her anger over the Vietnam War)
  • 1974 - Won the Hugo and Nebula Awards for her book The Dispossessed, which explored themes of anarchism and utopianism and made her the first ever person to win both awards for each of two books
  • 1977 - Refused a Nebula Award for her short story "The Diary of the Rose", in protest of the Science Fiction Writers of America's revocation of Stanislaw Lem's membership, stating that she felt reluctance to accept a award "for a story about political intolerance from a group that had just displayed political intolerance"
  • 1979 - Until 1994, wrote primarily for a younger audience, with children's books such as the Catwings series and novels for adolescents such as The Beginning Place
  • 2002 - Won the World Fantasy Award for The Other Wind, the final installment in the Earthsea series
  • 2009 - Resigned from the Authors Guild in protest of its endorsement of Google's book digitization project, criticizing the guild abandoning certain principles "to a corporation, on their terms, without a struggle"
  • 2018 - Died of a heart attack on January 22 at age 88

    • “We read books to find out who we are. What other people, real or imaginary, do and think and feel... is an essential guide to our understanding of what we ourselves are and may become.” ― Ursula K. LeGuin
